Antarctica

Antarctica is Earth’s southernmost continent, containing the geographic South Pole. It is the fifth-largest continent, covering about 14 million square kilometers, which is roughly 9.2% of the Earth's land surface. Despite its massive size, Antarctica has no permanent population and is not governed by any one country.

Instead, Antarctica is managed through the Antarctic Treaty System, an international agreement signed by over 50 countries. The treaty prohibits military activity, mineral mining, and supports scientific cooperation. Among the most active and influential countries in Antarctica are the United States, Russia, China, the United Kingdom, and Australia. These nations maintain significant research stations and contribute heavily to scientific studies in the region.

Antarctica is rich in natural resources, including coal, iron ore, oil, and natural gas, though these are currently protected from exploitation under the treaty. It also holds around 70% of the world’s fresh water, locked in its massive ice sheets, making it critical to the planet’s climate and sea levels.

Australia claims the largest territorial portion of Antarctica (the Australian Antarctic Territory), but these claims are not universally recognized due to the treaty’s rules. Antarctica remains a continent dedicated to peace, science, and environmental preservation.
